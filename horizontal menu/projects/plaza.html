Crowne Plaza

Ignition is an innovative new meeting and working experience, housed in the lobby of Crowne Plaza hotels that incorporates an integrated new suite of brand, space, furniture, service role, food and beverage offers, digital tools, and business strategy. The concept is designed to increase travelers productivity and launched with two pilots in Atlanta. It's now being rolled out across the Americas as part of a 200 million dollar investment.

Huddle spot with integrated tech and whiteboard wall


Ignition has everything from single-person Pods, to the Nooks and Huddles for four-person impromptu meetings, to the 10-person Apartment (bookable by the hour).

Apartment bookable by the hour


The Apartment is a meeting space designed to become a local business fixture in its own right, attracting people to meet, and work, irrespective of whether they're staying.

Process
Through qualitative research and interviews with guests we had found that the current image of the business traveler is outdated. Today's workers dress casual and are looking to work flexibly, collaboratively and creatively.

A warehouse build with prototypes of new spaces, furniture and tech helped inform our pilot.


Mix of publicly-private and private work spaces and supporting digital tools



We tested the apps and foam-core mockups of the custom furniture in the studio before the pilot so we could get in-context feedback and do tweaks.



Wireframes of Apartment booking flow



Captured wireframes for moving into design in sketch



Workstreams in sketch



We built 100+ seat sensors that we installed in all seats so we could track where people sat down and how long they stayed. This allowed us to first create a baseline of how the old space was used and helped us learn what types of areas people appreciated the most during the pilot.



Blank printed circuit boards, ready to be outfitted with sensors, batteries, and transmitters



We later realised we could use the sensors to trigger welcome messages on the iPads as you sat down, as well as food and beverages nudges in combination with time of day.



Proximity sensor under a hard plastic stool



Cameras and motion tracking software allowed us to learn how people moved through the space.



A day's worth of motion data from the baseline, superimposed over a still image of the lobby area
